Rajasthan Solar Policy Dashboard 2025

Insights into RERC Regulations & Comparative Analysis

Overview & Tariff Regulations 2025

RERC's 2025 regulations aim to enhance solar adoption in Rajasthan. The "Terms and Conditions for Determination of Tariff" Regulations, 2025 (effective Apr 1, 2025 - Mar 31, 2030) are central to this, providing a framework for power pricing, including for solar projects.

Promotion of Distributed Solar

Regulations acknowledge the need to promote distributed solar power, fostering decentralized generation.

Green Energy Incentives

General incentives for green energy adoption are outlined, which will benefit solar project development.

Revised PLF Formula

RERC indicated revisions to the Plant Load Factor (PLF) formula, potentially impacting solar project viability assessments.

Multi-Year Tariff (MYT) Principles

Adherence to MYT principles aims to provide long-term financial visibility and stability for solar developers.

Focus on Transparency

Emphasis on transparency in tariff determination and public participation in the tariff-setting process.

Green Energy Open Access (GEOA) Regulations 2025

Issued in May 2025, these regulations significantly ease the process for consumers to access green energy, including solar.

Eligibility Criteria

100 kW+ Load: Consumers with a contract demand or sanctioned load of 100 kW or more are eligible.

No Lower Limit for Captive: No minimum load for captive consumers.

Energy Storage Mandate

>5 MW Projects: New RE projects (incl. solar) > 5 MW must have energy storage systems.

Captive >100% Demand: Captive plants exceeding 100% of contract demand need BESS for a portion of excess capacity.

Aims to enhance grid reliability.

Banking Facility

For Captive Users: Permitted up to 25% of injected energy or 30% of DISCOM-supplied consumption (whichever is higher).

8% In-Kind Fee: Subject to an 8% in-kind banking charge.

Streamlined Process & Charges

Simplified application via a central portal.

Defined charges: transmission, wheeling.

CSS Capped: Cross-Subsidy Surcharge (CSS) is capped and waived for captive solar plants.

Scope of Green Energy

Broadened to include wind, solar, hydro, hybrid sources, and energy storage systems using only renewable energy.

Priority Allocation

Preference given to GEOA consumers over traditional open-access consumers, supporting national RE goals.

Rooftop Solar Boost (PM Surya Ghar Yojana)

In March 2025, RERC approved key relaxations under DREGS Regulations, 2021, to support the PM Surya Ghar Muft Bijli Yojana.

Waiver of Application Fee

Application fees for domestic rooftop solar installations are waived, reducing upfront costs for consumers.

Waiver of Security Deposit

Security deposits required for domestic rooftop solar are waived, further easing financial burden.

No Connection Agreement

The requirement for a formal connection agreement for domestic rooftop solar installations is removed to expedite the process.

Waiver of Meter Testing Charges

Meter testing charges for domestic consumers opting for rooftop solar are waived.

Objective

To effectively implement the PM Surya Ghar Muft Bijli Yojana and achieve state targets for rooftop solar installations.

State Policy Comparison

Rajasthan vs. Gujarat

Rooftop Solar Ecosystem:

Gujarat: More mature and extensive, benefiting from early and consistent policy support.

Rajasthan: New relaxations aim to catch up and accelerate rooftop adoption.

Policy Cycle:

Gujarat: Demonstrated pattern of regular policy updates adapting to market changes.

Rajasthan: 2025 regulations mark a significant update; consistent future revisions will be key.

Capacity Restrictions:

Gujarat: Often features no capacity restrictions for many project types.

Rajasthan: GEOA defines eligibility (e.g., 100kW for open access), rooftop relaxations are for domestic scale.

Land Allocation:

Gujarat: Specific models for large solar parks have facilitated rapid development.

Rajasthan: Recent land law changes (early 2024) are a new factor impacting project timelines and costs.

Rajasthan vs. Other Leading States (General Trends)

Rooftop Solar Policies:

Rajasthan: Recent waivers are very positive for domestic consumers. Capacity limits and net/gross metering specifics vary across states.

Many states have adopted net/gross metering, but Rajasthan's fee waivers are a strong incentive.

Open Access Charges:

Rajasthan: GEOA regulations with capped and waived CSS for captive solar are competitive. This is a critical factor where states differ significantly.

Makes RE procurement more attractive for C&I consumers.

Energy Storage Integration:

Rajasthan: Mandate for energy storage in new GEOA regulations is forward-looking and aligns with national trends for grid stability.

Some other states are also exploring or mandating storage, but Rajasthan's clarity is notable.

Incentives & Subsidies:

Rajasthan: Relies on central schemes (PM Surya Ghar) augmented by state-level regulatory relaxations and budgetary support.

Direct state capital subsidies or GBIs vary greatly among leading solar states.

Impact & Outlook

Potential Impact in Rajasthan

  • Accelerated Solar Adoption: New regulations are poised to significantly boost solar uptake, especially in rooftop and C&I open access segments.
  • Improved Investor Confidence: Clearer tariff frameworks and GEOA rules can enhance investor confidence.
  • Enhanced Grid Stability: Mandating energy storage is a crucial step towards managing intermittent renewable energy.
  • Consumer Empowerment: Easier open access and rooftop solar processes benefit consumers.

Rajasthan's Positioning

With these 2025 regulations, Rajasthan strengthens its position as a leading state in India's solar energy transition. The proactive measures, particularly in Green Energy Open Access and support for rooftop solar, place it among the more progressive states.

Effective and timely implementation of these policies, alongside addressing challenges like land acquisition complexities from recent land law changes, will be crucial for realizing the state's full solar potential and contributing to national renewable energy targets.

Key Challenges to Monitor

  • Land Acquisition: Navigating the impacts of new land laws from early 2024.
  • Grid Infrastructure: Ensuring robust grid infrastructure to support increased RE penetration.
  • DISCOM Financial Health: Continued financial stability of distribution companies is vital for PPA adherence.
  • Consistent Implementation: Ensuring smooth and consistent application of new rules across all levels.
